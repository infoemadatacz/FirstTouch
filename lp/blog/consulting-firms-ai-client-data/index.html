The Real Problem: What Happens When You Use ChatGPT on a Client Engagement

Let's be direct about what happens when a consultant pastes a client's financial model, strategic plan, or interview transcript into a standard AI tool.

That data leaves your control the moment you hit send. It is transmitted to the provider's servers — typically in the United States — where it is processed, temporarily stored, and in many cases used to improve future model versions. You have no visibility over who at the provider may access it, under what circumstances, or for how long it is retained.

Your client does not know this is happening. Their engagement letter did not contemplate it. Your NDA almost certainly prohibits it. And GDPR — which applies the moment any of that data involves an identifiable person — requires that you have a documented legal basis and a signed data processing agreement before any third party touches it.

Legal Exposure — Real Scenario

A senior consultant uses ChatGPT to summarise a target company's internal financial data during an M&A due diligence mandate. The data includes projections, key personnel compensation, and a pending restructuring. The AI provider experiences a security incident six months later. Under GDPR Article 33, the consultant's firm must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours. The M&A client was never told the data left their environment. The regulatory and reputational consequences are severe — and entirely avoidable.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the logical consequence of using general-purpose consumer AI tools on professional mandates that carry confidentiality obligations — and it is happening at firms of every size right now.

Why This Matters More for Consultants Than for Most Businesses

Most businesses hold their own data. When they process employee records or customer data through an AI tool, they are making a decision about their own risk. Consultants operate differently.

The data you hold at any given moment almost certainly belongs to someone else. Your clients have trusted you with information that defines their competitive position, their valuation, their negotiating strategy, or their most sensitive people decisions. That trust is the foundation of every retainer, every referral, and every long-term relationship your firm depends on.

The risk hierarchy for consulting firms looks like this:

  • Breach of client confidentiality — the most immediate commercial risk. Most engagement letters include explicit confidentiality provisions. Unauthorised transfer of client data to a third-party processor may constitute a breach, regardless of whether harm results.
  • GDPR liability as data controller — if client data includes personal information about their employees, customers, or any identifiable individuals, you become a data controller the moment you hold it. Processing that data through a non-compliant AI tool means processing without a valid legal basis under Article 6.
  • Article 28 processor obligations — even where you act as processor rather than controller, GDPR requires a documented DPA with every sub-processor. Standard consumer AI tools are not designed to be GDPR-compliant sub-processors.
  • Sector-specific regulations — for clients in financial services, healthcare, or critical infrastructure, additional rules apply. Using AI to process their data may inadvertently pull your firm into regulated activity.
  • Cross-engagement contamination — if the same AI tool is used across multiple client engagements, there is a genuine risk that information from one engagement influences outputs in another. Some providers use conversation history to personalise responses. The implications for conflict management are serious.
The Quiet Competitor Risk

AI providers train models on diverse data sources. If your competitor's consultant uses the same tool and asks similar questions — about market sizing in your client's sector, or competitive positioning in their category — there is no guarantee that information from your client's engagement does not eventually shape a response to theirs. The architecture of shared AI infrastructure makes this a structural concern, not a paranoid one.

The GDPR Framework Consultants Must Understand

Most consultants are aware of GDPR in the abstract. Few have mapped it carefully against their AI tool usage. Here is the framework that matters.

Data Controller vs. Data Processor

When you hold client data that contains personal information — an employee list, a customer survey, an organisational chart with named individuals — you are a data controller for that data. You determine the purpose and means of processing. This means the full weight of GDPR applies to how you handle it, not just your client.

If you pass that data to an AI tool, that tool becomes a data processor on your behalf. Under Article 28, every data processor must operate under a written contract that specifies exactly what processing will occur, where, for how long, and under what security conditions. Consumer AI tools do not provide this. Their terms of service are not data processing agreements — they run in the opposite direction, granting the provider broad rights to use your inputs.

Article 28 — What a Compliant DPA Must Include

  • 1 Processing only on documented instructions — the processor may only process personal data in accordance with your written instructions. Generic AI tools process data to serve their own product development goals.
  • 2 Confidentiality obligations on all personnel — everyone with access to the data at the processor must be bound by confidentiality. AI providers' support and safety review teams are not bound by your client's confidentiality requirements.
  • 3 Documented security measures — the processor must implement technical and organisational security measures appropriate to the risk. You must be able to document what those measures are to demonstrate compliance.
  • 4 Sub-processor approval — the processor must not engage sub-processors without your prior written consent. AI providers use extensive infrastructure chains — cloud providers, CDNs, safety review vendors — none of which are disclosed to you or approved by your clients.
  • 5 Data subject rights support — the processor must assist you in handling data subject access requests, erasure requests, and other rights. AI providers' data retention architectures make this practically impossible.
  • 6 Deletion or return of data on termination — all personal data must be deleted or returned at the end of the processing relationship. AI tools do not provide client-specific deletion guarantees.

International Transfers

Almost all major AI tools are operated by US-based companies and process data on US infrastructure. Transferring personal data from the EU or UK to the US requires a valid transfer mechanism under GDPR Chapter V — either Standard Contractual Clauses, adequacy decisions, or Binding Corporate Rules. Consumer AI tools' standard terms do not reliably provide this. The EU-US Data Privacy Framework addresses some transfers, but its durability has been contested and enforcement is active.

For UK firms, the UK GDPR applies parallel requirements. For clients based in Germany or France, DPA authorities in both jurisdictions have been among the most active in enforcement action against AI tool providers.

What "Data Sovereign AI" Means in Practice for a Consulting Firm

Data sovereignty for consulting firms means one thing: the data you process for each client stays within an environment you control, isolated from other client engagements, and never accessible to a third-party AI provider's infrastructure.

In practice, this means running AI models on your own server or a dedicated private cloud environment — not on shared infrastructure operated by a consumer AI company. The model runs locally. Client documents are processed locally. Outputs are generated locally. Nothing leaves the environment.

This is architecturally straightforward and increasingly affordable. It does not require a data centre or an engineering team. It requires the right setup — which is precisely what professional AI implementation firms provide.

Client-Level Isolation

A well-implemented private AI environment maintains complete separation between client engagements. Documents loaded for Client A are never accessible when working on Client B. There is no shared context, no cross-contamination, and a full audit trail of every document accessed and every interaction processed. This is the standard your clients would expect if they asked.

What Good AI Governance Looks Like for a Consulting Firm

Getting AI governance right for a consulting firm is not about writing a policy and hoping people follow it. It requires architecture that makes compliant behaviour the default — not the extra effort.

Policy Elements

  • Approved tools register — a defined list of approved AI tools, what data classifications each tool may process, and what client consent requirements apply before use
  • Client data classification — a clear framework distinguishing between non-sensitive internal tasks (approved for any tool), engagement data without personal information (approved for enterprise tools with DPA), and sensitive client data (approved for private AI only)
  • Engagement-level AI disclosure — a standard clause for engagement letters advising clients of AI tool usage and data processing arrangements
  • Incident response procedure — a documented process for what happens if data is inadvertently processed through an unapproved tool, including GDPR notification obligations

Technical Architecture

  • Private AI environment — AI model running on infrastructure you control, with no external data transmission
  • Client workspace isolation — separate context and document stores per engagement, preventing cross-contamination
  • Audit logging — complete record of documents accessed, prompts submitted, and outputs generated per engagement
  • Access controls — role-based access ensuring only the team working on a specific engagement can access that engagement's AI environment

Frequently Asked Questions

Can consultants use ChatGPT to analyse client documents?
Not safely. When you paste client data into ChatGPT or similar tools, that data is sent to and processed on OpenAI's servers. It may be used to train future models, stored for extended periods, and is subject to US jurisdiction. Most client engagement letters do not authorise this. You risk breaching confidentiality obligations, GDPR data processor rules, and potentially NDA terms.
What is a data processor agreement and does it matter for AI tools?
Under GDPR Article 28, any third party that processes personal data on your behalf must sign a Data Processing Agreement (DPA). Standard ChatGPT or consumer AI tools are not designed as GDPR-compliant data processors — they have their own terms of service and data retention policies that conflict with your obligations as a controller. This matters enormously when client data contains employee details, financial information, or any identifiable person data.
How do I use AI for consulting work without risking client confidentiality?
Use an AI system where the model runs on infrastructure you control — either on your own servers or a private cloud environment with documented data boundaries. The model should never share context between client engagements, data should not be used for training, and you should be able to demonstrate to clients exactly where their data is processed and stored.
Can I just use the enterprise version of ChatGPT?
ChatGPT Enterprise offers stronger data protections than the consumer version and claims not to use your inputs for training. However, data still transits to and is processed on Microsoft Azure infrastructure in the US, which creates jurisdictional concerns for EU-based clients under GDPR's international transfer rules. It also does not prevent cross-contamination risks if the same account is used across multiple client projects. For most consulting engagements with sensitive mandates, this is insufficient.
What AI tasks give consultants the biggest productivity gains?
Based on consulting firm deployments: (1) drafting first versions of reports, proposals, and slide narratives from raw notes — saves 3–5 hours per deliverable; (2) synthesising interview transcripts into themes — saves 4–8 hours per discovery phase; (3) gap analysis against frameworks (e.g. ISO, sector benchmarks) using the client's own documents; (4) preparing board briefings and executive summaries; (5) proposal writing against RFP criteria.
Is there a compliance risk even if the client doesn't find out?
Yes. Under GDPR, a data breach or unauthorised processing must be reported regardless of whether the affected party discovers it themselves. If an AI vendor suffers a breach and your client data was in their system, you are legally obligated to notify the relevant supervisory authority within 72 hours and potentially notify affected individuals. The regulatory exposure exists independently of client discovery.
