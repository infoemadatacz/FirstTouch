The SRA Obligations That Every AI Decision Must Respect

The SRA Code of Conduct for Solicitors and for Firms contains several Principles and rules that directly govern how AI tools can be used in legal practice. Three are particularly relevant to any firm considering or already using AI for client work.

SRA P.6
You keep the affairs of current and former clients confidential unless disclosure is required or permitted by law or the client consents
This is absolute. There is no carve-out for "accidentally" transmitting client information to a third-party AI platform. Any AI tool that processes client matter data on external servers — without client consent and a valid legal basis — risks placing a solicitor in breach of this Principle, regardless of whether disclosure ever occurs or whether any harm results. The breach is the transmission, not the consequence.
SRA P.7
You act in the best interests of each client
Using an AI tool that could expose client data to competitors, third parties, or regulators — even inadvertently — may not constitute acting in a client's best interests. Senior partners increasingly treat AI tool selection as a client care decision, not merely an IT decision. The question "does this tool protect my client's interests?" must be answered before deployment, not after an incident.
COC 4.4
You must not make misleading or inaccurate statements to clients
AI-generated output must be reviewed for accuracy before it reaches a client. The SRA has explicitly stated that the use of AI does not reduce a solicitor's personal responsibility for the accuracy of advice or documents produced. Supervision of AI-generated output is a professional obligation, not a best practice. An AI assistant that produces an inaccurate legal summary and a solicitor who sends it to a client without checking it are together responsible for whatever follows.

Legal Professional Privilege and the Third-Party Problem

Legal professional privilege — both legal advice privilege and litigation privilege — protects confidential communications between solicitor and client from compelled disclosure. It is one of the most fundamental protections in the English legal system, and it depends entirely on the communications remaining confidential.

Privilege requires confidentiality. Communications that have been disclosed to a third party, or that are no longer confidential, may lose privilege protection. The legal position on whether transmitting client communications to an AI platform constitutes disclosure to a third party — sufficient to affect privilege — is not yet fully settled in English law. But the risk is real, courts are beginning to engage with it in discovery contexts, and the direction of travel is unfavourable to firms that have transmitted privileged communications to external platforms.

The practical test is straightforward: if an opposing party in litigation became aware that a solicitor had been processing client communications through a public AI platform, would they seek to argue that privilege had been waived? For an increasing number of aggressive commercial litigators, the answer is yes. The question for law firm partners is whether that argument should ever be available.

How AI deployment architecture affects privilege risk

✗
Public AI platform (ChatGPT, Copilot standard tiers): Client advice is transmitted to a third-party server. The platform provider processes the communication. Depending on terms, may retain it for varying periods. The third-party access argument is available to an opposing party who discovers the use. Privilege may be challengeable. SRA breach risk is significant on any matter-specific use.
✗
Enterprise SaaS AI (improved terms, limited isolation): Data remains within the provider's infrastructure under contractual controls. Better than consumer tier — but client data still leaves the firm's environment. Privilege argument is stronger, but not watertight. The provider's internal access, sub-processor chain, and data residency practices remain relevant risk factors.
✓
On-premise private AI (dedicated server, zero external processing): Communication never leaves the firm's controlled infrastructure. No third-party access — by architecture, not just by contract. Privilege is not affected by AI use. This is legally equivalent to a solicitor using the firm's own document management system or word processor. There is no novel third-party disclosure argument available.

The UK GDPR Obligations That Apply to Client Data in AI

Law firms hold significant volumes of personal data: client personal details, counterparty information, witness statements, HR records, and litigation documents containing highly sensitive individual information. Processing any of this data using an AI tool creates obligations under UK GDPR that most firms have not fully mapped.

The Article 28 gap most law firms are sitting in right now: UK GDPR Article 28 requires a signed Data Processing Agreement with any third party that processes personal data on a firm's behalf — before processing begins. The standard consumer and SME tiers of ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, and similar platforms do not provide DPAs on request. Even enterprise tiers require specific configuration and negotiation that most firms have not completed. A firm whose fee-earners are using public AI platforms for client work almost certainly does not have Article 28 coverage for that processing. That is an ICO enforcement risk, a client notification obligation if a breach occurs, and a professional conduct exposure — simultaneously.

Beyond the DPA, firms must consider their retention and deletion obligations. Client personal data processed through an external AI platform may be retained by the provider under their own terms for periods that are inconsistent with the firm's matter file retention policy. A client exercising their UK GDPR Article 17 right to erasure cannot be satisfied if the firm cannot confirm — let alone demonstrate — whether their data has been deleted from an AI provider's infrastructure and sub-processor chain.

There is also a data minimisation question. UK GDPR Article 5(1)(c) requires that personal data processed is limited to what is necessary for the stated purpose. Pasting entire client files into an AI platform to answer a narrow question is difficult to defend as minimal processing. A private AI deployment, configured with the firm's own matter data within its own infrastructure, resolves this neatly: the data is already there, accessible for the specific purpose without any new transmission event.

What an SRA-Ready AI Policy for a Law Firm Must Contain

The SRA has made clear that firms should have documented policies governing AI use, and that inspectors should be able to review those policies on request. A policy that satisfies both SRA expectation and UK GDPR accountability principles contains four components — none of which can be substituted with a general statement that "AI use must be appropriate."

Scope definition: Which tasks may use AI assistance, which tasks may not, and what data categories are permitted to be processed by AI tools under each permitted task category. This is not a general prohibition or a general permission — it is a mapped set of use cases with defined data handling rules for each. A policy that says "fee-earners may use AI for research and drafting" is not a compliant policy. A policy that says "AI tools approved for task type X may be used with data category Y, subject to conditions Z" is.

Tool approval list: An explicit list of approved AI tools and the conditions under which each is approved for use. For private AI deployed by the firm, this includes the server hosting details, the DPA reference, and the DPIA outcome. For any external tools approved for non-client-data tasks, it includes the acceptable use policy review and DPA status. Any tool not on the approved list is by default not approved.

Supervision requirements: How AI-generated output is to be reviewed before it reaches a client or is used in proceedings. Who is responsible for that review at each seniority level. How the review is recorded on the matter file. This addresses the SRA's explicit position that AI use does not reduce personal professional responsibility for output accuracy. A firm that cannot demonstrate a supervision process is exposed to regulatory criticism regardless of the quality of the AI tool.

Incident and breach protocol: What happens when a fee-earner accidentally uses an unapproved tool for client matter work. How the incident is assessed against the ICO's notification threshold. Whether client notification is required and who makes that decision. How the matter file is updated to reflect the incident. And what process prevents recurrence — not just documentation of what happened.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can UK solicitors use AI tools for client work under SRA rules?
Yes, but with strict conditions. SRA Principle 6 requires solicitors to keep client affairs confidential. Any AI tool that transmits client information to external servers — without an appropriate Data Processing Agreement and client disclosure — risks breaching that obligation. Solicitors can use AI compliantly by deploying private, on-premise systems where client data never leaves the firm's controlled infrastructure.
Does using AI for legal work affect legal professional privilege?
Potentially yes. Legal professional privilege attaches to confidential communications between solicitor and client for the purpose of giving legal advice. If those communications are transmitted to a third-party AI platform, the confidentiality of the communication may be compromised, which can affect privilege. On-premise AI deployment avoids this issue entirely because the communication stays within the firm's environment — no third party has access by architecture.
What AI tasks can solicitors perform without client data compliance risk?
Tasks that do not involve client-identifiable information carry minimal compliance risk: drafting precedent documents, researching general legal principles, generating non-matter-specific template letters, creating training materials, and producing internal policy documents. For tasks involving matter-specific client data — drafting client advice, summarising client documents, reviewing contracts — a data-sovereign deployment is required.
What does the SRA say about law firms using AI?
The SRA has published guidance noting that the use of AI does not reduce a solicitor's professional obligations. Firms remain responsible for the accuracy of AI-generated output, must maintain appropriate supervision, and must not use tools that compromise client confidentiality. The SRA has also flagged that firms should have policies governing AI use and should be able to demonstrate compliance on request during regulatory visits.
How quickly can a law firm deploy a compliant AI system?
A standard First Touch deployment for a law firm takes 10 to 14 days from agreement to go-live. This includes server provisioning in a UK data centre, DPA execution, DPIA completion, AI use policy drafting, and knowledge base configuration with the firm's precedents, practice areas, and workflow templates. The full compliance documentation pack is available on the day of deployment — not weeks later.
Does using AI for legal drafting constitute unauthorised practice or reduce the quality of legal advice?
No — provided supervision requirements are met. AI drafts are starting points, not final products. The solicitor reviewing, amending, and approving the output is providing the legal advice. The AI is accelerating the drafting process, not replacing professional judgment. The SRA's position is consistent with this: AI does not reduce the solicitor's responsibility, which means the solicitor must exercise judgment over the output before it is relied upon.
